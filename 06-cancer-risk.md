# Cancer Risk Reduction

## Overview

Coffee is one of the most heavily studied dietary exposures in cancer epidemiology, with more than 1,000 human and animal studies reviewed by the World Health Organization's International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC). The contemporary picture is broadly reassuring: for most cancers coffee is either neutral or *associated* with reduced risk, and the most consistent inverse associations are for liver and endometrial cancer. Crucially, nearly all of this evidence is **observational** (prospective cohorts, case-control studies, and meta-analyses of them). It establishes consistent associations, not proven causation, because randomized trials with cancer endpoints are not feasible. Authorities such as Harvard's T.H. Chan School of Public Health and IARC conclude that moderate coffee drinking does not increase cancer risk and may be associated with lower risk for several sites. As detailed below, newer causal-inference (Mendelian randomization) studies have tempered several of these associations, so the inverse associations should be read as promising but not established.

## Key Findings

**Liver cancer (most consistent observational evidence).** Dose-response meta-analyses of cohort studies show the highest coffee consumers have roughly half the *observed* risk of liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma) versus non/occasional drinkers, with a pooled relative risk (RR) near 0.55 (95% CI 0.44-0.67). The association is dose-graded across studies. However, this is an observational correlation, not demonstrated causation: people with early or chronic liver disease often cut back on coffee (reverse causation), and only about 2 of 20 cohorts fully adjusted for major confounders such as smoking, alcohol, diabetes, and BMI. Notably, a 2025 meta-analysis of Mendelian randomization studies (Choi et al., *Gut and Liver*) found **no significant causal association** (OR 0.92, 95% CI 0.58-1.47), concluding the observational inverse association may have been overestimated and that coffee may not causally lower HCC risk.

**Liver cancer dose-response (observational pattern).** Within individual dose-response meta-analyses of cohort data, observed risk is lower at higher intake; one widely cited analysis (Kennedy et al., 2017, *BMJ Open*) reported roughly 25% lower risk at 1 cup/day, ~38% at 2 cups, ~45% at 3 cups, and ~51% at 4 cups/day. This is a non-linear, diminishing-returns curve rather than a constant per-cup effect, and the figures describe what cohorts *observe*, not a proven causal reduction. Consistent with the caveat above, Mendelian randomization analyses have not confirmed a protective causal effect on digestive cancers, and no randomized trials exist.

**Endometrial cancer (probable, observational).** Pooled prospective data indicate observed risk about 20% lower per 4 cups/day, and ~24% lower for postmenopausal endometrial cancer; high versus no consumption gives an RR near 0.80 (Je & Giovannucci, 2012; later dose-response meta-analyses). These are observational associations graded only as **probable** (not convincing) by WCRF/AICR. A 2023 two-sample Mendelian randomization study found **no causal effect** of coffee or caffeine on endometrial cancer risk across subtypes, and because decaffeinated coffee shows a similar association to caffeinated, the effect cannot be attributed to caffeine and the mechanism remains unclear. Associations in cohort data appear stronger in women with BMI >=25 and those never on hormone therapy.

**Colorectal cancer (moderate/probable).** Umbrella reviews rate coffee as having a *probable* protective association. A large population-based cohort reported about a 17% lower risk among coffee drinkers, though results across studies are less consistent than for liver or endometrium, and the association is observational.

**Prostate cancer (modest, borderline).** A 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis (*BMJ Open*) found highest versus lowest consumption RR 0.91 (95% CI 0.84-0.98), with risk falling about 2.5% per 2 cups/day. The signal is small and borderline: the upper confidence bound (0.98) sits close to the null, heterogeneity was moderate (I²=53%), and the benefit did **not** extend to advanced (RR 0.88, 95% CI 0.71-1.09) or fatal (RR 0.84, 95% CI 0.66-1.08) disease. Mendelian randomization studies found no causal association, and WCRF/AICR grades the overall evidence as "limited - no conclusion."

**Skin cancer (modest, caffeine-specific).** Two large U.S. cohorts (Nurses' Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study) found inverse associations between caffeinated coffee/total caffeine and basal cell carcinoma. A nonmelanoma skin cancer dose-response meta-analysis reported summary RRs of 0.96 (1 cup), 0.92 (1-2 cups), 0.89 (2-3 cups), and 0.81 (>3 cups/day). For melanoma, the NIH-AARP cohort (447,357 participants; 2,904 incident cases over ~10.5 years median follow-up) and meta-analyses found a modest inverse association limited to *caffeinated* (not decaffeinated) coffee. These remain observational associations.

**Breast cancer (largely neutral).** Most evidence shows no meaningful change in overall breast cancer risk; some umbrella reviews list a possible small decreased risk, but the association is weak and inconsistent.

## Strength of Evidence

All findings above derive from **observational** research and therefore cannot prove that coffee *causes* lower cancer risk; residual confounding (especially smoking) and reverse causation remain possible. The most consistent inverse associations in cohort data are for **liver** and **endometrial** cancer, where observed effect sizes are large and dose-graded - but for both, Mendelian randomization (a genetic, more causally informative design) has so far failed to confirm a protective causal effect, so these should be described as probable, not established. Colorectal and prostate associations are weaker still; skin and breast effects are modest or neutral.

The classification history is instructive. In **1991 IARC classified coffee as Group 2B** ("possibly carcinogenic," driven by bladder cancer signals). In **June 2016, after reviewing >1,000 studies, IARC's Working Group downgraded coffee to Group 3** ("not classifiable as to carcinogenicity"), stating the earlier bladder association *may* have reflected inadequate control for tobacco smoking. Group 3 means the evidence is inadequate to decide - not that coffee is proven safe or protective; bladder cancer in fact remained the one site rated "inadequate evidence" even in 2016. The same broad review noted observed reduced risks for liver and endometrial cancer. Separately, IARC classified **drinking very hot beverages (above ~65°C) as Group 2A** ("probably carcinogenic"), an effect attributed to thermal injury of the esophagus, not to coffee itself.

A 2017 *BMJ* umbrella review (Poole et al.) of 201 observational meta-analyses found high versus low consumption associated with ~18% lower incident cancer overall (RR 0.82, 95% CI 0.74-0.89). Two important caveats: the review's "3-4 cups/day greatest benefit" finding applied to **all-cause and cardiovascular mortality**, not to cancer specifically; and the great majority of associations were graded "very low" quality, with the authors stressing that randomized trials are needed to judge causation and that people should not start drinking coffee for health reasons. A later dedicated umbrella review (Zhao et al., 2020) found the broad cancer-protective signal is not robust - only liver and endometrial cancer reached "highly suggestive" evidence, with most cancer sites showing no significant association.

## Mechanisms

Coffee is a complex mixture of bioactive compounds that plausibly counter carcinogenesis. **Chlorogenic acids and other polyphenols** provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity, reducing oxidative DNA damage. **Caffeine** can promote apoptosis and inhibit proliferation in damaged cells (a leading hypothesis for the caffeine-specific skin-cancer signal involving UV-damaged keratinocytes/melanocytes). Coffee **improves insulin sensitivity and lowers circulating insulin/IGF-1**, relevant to colorectal and endometrial cancers. It may also **lower bioavailable estrogen and sex-hormone exposure**, one proposed (but unproven) explanation for endometrial associations - though the similar association seen with decaffeinated coffee leaves the mechanism uncertain. For the liver, coffee consistently **lowers liver enzymes and is associated with reduced hepatic fibrosis, steatosis, and cirrhosis**, and upregulates detoxifying enzymes - the most developed mechanistic story of any site. The diterpenes **cafestol and kahweol** show chemoprotective, enzyme-modulating effects in laboratory models. These are biologically plausible pathways, but plausibility does not by itself establish a causal cancer benefit in humans.

## Practical Notes

- In observational data, **3-4 cups/day** is the intake associated with the greatest benefit for overall mortality and cardiovascular disease; for cancer specifically the evidence comes mostly from high-versus-low comparisons rather than a clear cancer-specific optimum. Benefits plateau and excessive intake offers no added protection.
- These are **population-level associations**, not guarantees, and causal-inference studies have not confirmed several of them; coffee should not be started or treated as a cancer-prevention "treatment."
- Let **very hot beverages cool below ~65°C** before drinking to avoid the Group 2A esophageal thermal-injury risk.
- For skin and some melanoma analyses, the signal is often **strongest for caffeinated coffee**; decaffeinated coffee shows weaker or null associations (though for endometrial cancer the association is similar for both).
- Pregnancy is a notable exception where moderation/limitation is advised for non-cancer reasons.
