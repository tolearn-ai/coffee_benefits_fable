# Coffee & Health: A Fact-Checked Report Series

This is an index to a 13-part report series on the purported health benefits of coffee. Each report was **adversarially fact-checked**: headline claims were traced to primary studies, effect sizes reported faithfully (with confidence intervals and heterogeneity), and—crucially—observational *associations* were separated from *causal* evidence. Where Mendelian randomization, randomized trials, or contradictory studies undercut a popular claim, the reports say so plainly rather than cherry-picking favorable numbers.

## Evidence at a glance

Ranked by the strength of evidence drawn from the reports themselves:

- **Strong (causal, randomized-trial evidence).** Acute athletic/physical performance, caffeine as an analgesic adjuvant for headache/migraine, and short-term alertness/reaction-time gains. These come from blinded, placebo-controlled RCTs, so causation—not just correlation—is supported. Effects are nonetheless modest.
- **Moderate (large, consistent observational; partial causal support).** Type 2 diabetes, liver disease, cardiovascular disease, and all-cause mortality. These show big, dose-responsive, geographically diverse associations, but causation is unproven and several are tempered by null Mendelian-randomization or RCT findings.
- **Emerging / weaker (observational, contested, or modest).** Depression and mood, most cancers (liver and endometrial strongest), stroke, respiratory outcomes, Parkinson's, and the "other benefits" (gout, kidney stones, gallstones, MS). Dementia/Alzheimer's evidence is genuinely unsettled.

## Caveats

Benefits are not a license for unlimited consumption. Coffee can disrupt sleep, provoke anxiety or panic in susceptible people, acutely raise blood pressure, elevate LDL when unfiltered (cafestol), blunt iron absorption, and cause genuine dependence. Pregnant people and those with anxiety, uncontrolled hypertension, GERD, iron deficiency, or slow caffeine metabolism have clear reasons to moderate. See **[11 – Caveats & Limits](11-caveats-and-limits.md)** for the full picture and safe-intake guidance (~400 mg/day for most healthy adults; ~200 mg/day in pregnancy).

## Closing note

Most human evidence here is **observational**: it shows association, not proven cause and effect, and is vulnerable to confounding and reverse causation. Where benefits appear, they assume **moderate consumption** (roughly 3–4 cups/day) **without heavy added sugar, syrup, or cream**, which can erase the very benefits these studies describe. Non-drinkers need not start coffee for their health.
