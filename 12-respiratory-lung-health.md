# Respiratory & Lung Health Benefits

## Overview

Coffee's principal bioactive compound, caffeine, is a methylxanthine — the same chemical family as theophylline, a long-established asthma drug. Because of this kinship, caffeine produces a genuine but small and short-lived bronchodilator effect, modestly opening the airways for a few hours after a dose. Importantly, this improvement is at the margins of what is usually considered clinically significant, and caffeine is not a recognized treatment for asthma. Beyond this acute pharmacology, observational studies link habitual moderate coffee drinking to somewhat better lung function, lower asthma prevalence, and lower respiratory mortality — but these are associations, not demonstrated cause and effect. The evidence is a mix of a few small randomized trials (for the acute bronchodilator effect) and large observational datasets (for the chronic associations), and the two should not be conflated. Smoking strongly confounds and modifies many of these relationships.

## Key Findings

**Acute bronchodilation (randomized trials).** A Cochrane systematic review (Welsh & Bara 2010, CD001112.pub2) examined seven small cross-over randomized trials in 75 people with mild-to-moderate asthma. The lung-function pooling, however, drew on only six of those trials and 55 participants (the seventh trial, with 20 people, measured exhaled nitric oxide rather than airway function). In that subset, caffeine improved FEV1 versus placebo for up to two hours (standardized mean difference 0.72; 95% CI 0.25–1.20), which the review translates into roughly a 5% mean difference in FEV1. Mid-expiratory flow rates (FEF25–75) improved for up to four hours, but the FEV1 benefit reached statistical significance only out to about two hours. Doses ranged from about 3.5 to 10 mg/kg — described by the review as roughly one to five cups of coffee. Cochrane itself characterizes the improvement as "small and at the margins of what would normally be considered clinically significant," found no evidence on symptoms or quality of life, and states caffeine is not a recognized asthma treatment. The review is from 2010, is no longer being updated, and rests on very small, dated studies.

**Habitual intake and lung function (cross-sectional analysis).** In the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) study of 10,658 adults, a cross-sectional analysis of 1987–1989 baseline data found FVC and FEV1 about 2–3% higher in people drinking four or more cups daily versus rare/never drinkers — but only among never and former smokers, with no benefit in current smokers. Because this is a single cross-sectional snapshot, it can only suggest "a possible beneficial effect," not a longitudinal or causal one, and residual confounding (diet, socioeconomic status, lifestyle) is plausible. The signal was driven largely by former smokers; among never smokers the FEV1 trend was not statistically significant (p_trend = 0.07; FVC p_trend = 0.04). The finding rests on one ~17-year-old study that has not been clearly replicated, and newer prospective evidence is mixed: the Korean Anseong-Ansan cohort (n=6,221, 20-year follow-up) reported that high instant-coffee intake was associated with *increased* COPD risk (HR 1.46) and lower FEV1/FVC in heavy smokers.

**Asthma prevalence (meta-analysis, Mendelian randomization).** A 2025 dose-response meta-analysis (Wang et al., Food & Nutrition Research) reported an inverse association — specifically for ground coffee — with asthma (pooled OR 0.86; 95% CI 0.82–0.91), with 2–3 cups/day linked to the lowest risk. This estimate should be read cautiously: it pooled only four publications, the 671,417-participant total is dominated by a single cohort (UK Biobank, ~424,725 people), heterogeneity was substantial (I² = 66.2%), and study designs were mixed (some cross-sectional, which cannot establish temporality). It is a single, un-replicated paper, and that same study's Mendelian randomization estimate was near-null (OR 0.982; 95% CI 0.972–0.992), so a causal protective effect is not established. Separately, NHANES data found people in the highest urinary paraxanthine quartile had 53% lower odds of asthma, an association consistent with — but not proof that — caffeine metabolites (theophylline, paraxanthine) contribute.

**Respiratory mortality (cohorts).** Large prospective cohorts (NEJM 2012; Circulation 2015's three US cohorts) reported inverse associations between coffee drinking and death from respiratory disease. A UK Biobank analysis found that combined moderate coffee and tea intake was associated with lower respiratory-disease mortality (HR 0.69; 95% CI 0.57–0.83). These are observational associations, not evidence of cause and effect. Notably, some of the apparent respiratory-mortality signal weakens or disappears when restricted to never-smokers, underscoring residual confounding by smoking.

**COPD — genuinely mixed.** A 2018 systematic review (Alfaro et al., Clinical Respiratory Journal) concluded coffee was associated with reduced respiratory mortality and, in one study, better lung function, but found no consistent association with COPD progression. Some studies report higher COPD risk with heavy instant-coffee intake among heavy smokers. COPD evidence is therefore conflicting and should not be summarized as a clear benefit.

## Strength of Evidence

The acute bronchodilator effect is causally demonstrated (randomized trials, not mere association) but small, short-lived, and of marginal clinical relevance — and graded as low-certainty given only a handful of tiny, dated studies (75 people total, 55 in the lung-function analyses). It is clinically relevant mainly as a confounder: patients are advised to withhold caffeine for at least four hours before spirometry or methacholine challenge, because caffeine can mask airflow obstruction and slightly lower exhaled nitric oxide.

The chronic associations (better lung function, lower asthma, lower respiratory mortality) are observational only and cannot establish causation: coffee drinkers differ from non-drinkers in smoking, diet, socioeconomic status, and reverse causation (sick people cut back). Smoking is a particularly strong modifier — benefits concentrate in never/former smokers and vanish in, or reverse for, current smokers. Mendelian randomization signals are weak and near-null, supportive at best and not definitive. No major guideline recommends coffee as a respiratory therapy.

## Mechanisms

Caffeine is structurally and pharmacologically related to theophylline. At ordinary dietary doses its airway smooth-muscle relaxation is driven chiefly by antagonism of adenosine receptors — caffeine is a non-selective adenosine antagonist (A1, A2A, A2B, A3), with A1/A2B blockade of mast-cell mediator release (histamine, leukotrienes) most relevant to the airways. The often-cited phosphodiesterase (PDE) inhibition pathway, which raises intracellular cyclic AMP, requires concentrations roughly 20- to 100-fold higher (near-toxic serum levels) and is widely regarded as negligible at normal dietary intake; pharmacologists consider the idea that caffeine acts mainly via PDE inhibition untenable. The theophylline analogy is also incomplete: theophylline's principal anti-asthma action at clinical concentrations is now understood to be anti-inflammatory, via histone deacetylase (HDAC2) activation, a mechanism distinct from PDE inhibition. Coffee's polyphenolic antioxidants (chlorogenic acids) may additionally protect lung tissue from oxidative stress, a plausible but unproven contributor to the cohort lung-function findings.

## Practical Notes

- The bronchodilator effect is real but weak, temporary, and at the margins of clinical significance — caffeine is not a substitute for inhalers or prescribed asthma controllers, and is not a recognized asthma treatment.
- Withhold caffeine for at least four hours before lung-function tests (spirometry, FeNO, bronchial challenge) to avoid skewed results.
- Associations are strongest in never/former smokers; coffee does not offset smoking damage (and may track with worse outcomes in heavy smokers), and quitting smoking dwarfs any possible coffee benefit.
- Moderate intake (about 2–4 cups/day) tracks with the most favorable respiratory associations; very high intake shows no added benefit. EFSA considers up to 400 mg caffeine/day (about 4 cups) safe for most adults.
