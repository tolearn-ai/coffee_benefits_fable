# Other Documented Benefits

## Overview

Beyond its well-studied roles in liver, cardiovascular, and metabolic health, coffee has accumulated a scattered but interesting body of evidence linking it to lower risk of several other conditions: gout, kidney stones, chronic kidney disease, gallstones, multiple sclerosis (MS), and melanoma, plus possible effects on DNA integrity, post-surgical bowel recovery, hearing, and day-to-day alertness. The quality of evidence varies enormously across these domains — from a few randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and Mendelian randomization (MR) studies that approach causal inference, down to single cross-sectional surveys. Most associations are observational and should be read as "linked to," not "proven to cause." Even the randomized and genetic studies here are mostly small, single, or contested. This report summarizes the strongest findings in each area and flags where the certainty is weaker than headline figures imply.

## Key Findings

**Gout and uric acid.** In the UK Biobank prospective cohort (447,658 participants, 3,053 incident gout cases, median ~13-year follow-up), gout risk was lower with higher coffee intake in a dose-dependent pattern: hazard ratio (HR) 0.70 (95% CI 0.63–0.77) at 2–3 cups/day and 0.60 (0.47–0.77) at >6 cups/day versus none — roughly a 40% lower risk at the highest intake. Both caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee were associated with lower risk. The dose-response plateaued around 3 cups/day, and the >6 cups/day estimate rests on few cases (wide CI), so the apparent extra benefit at very high intake is imprecise. A 2025 meta-analysis reported a protective pooled odds ratio for hyperuricemia/gout (around 0.59 for gout), though with very high between-study heterogeneity (I²~88%) and a literature dominated by cross-sectional studies. This is a single observational cohort: the authors state the association "cannot be interpreted as causal," and the relevant MR evidence is population-specific (a causal protective effect was robust in Japanese cohorts but not statistically significant in Europeans, P=0.092).

**Kidney stones.** A single Mendelian randomization study (American Journal of Kidney Diseases, 2021) using UK Biobank and FinnGen data estimated an odds ratio (OR) of 0.60 (0.46–0.79) per genetically predicted 50% increase in coffee consumption and 0.81 (0.69–0.94) per 80-mg increase in caffeine — which the authors interpreted as possible causal protection. This interpretation should be treated cautiously: the study has not been independently replicated, the coffee genetic instrument is heavily influenced by pleiotropic caffeine-metabolism genes (CYP1A2, AHR, rs1260326) so the signal partly reflects caffeine metabolism rather than coffee intake itself, and a systematic review of coffee MR studies concluded most such associations "lack robust evidence." No randomized trial exists, and the "50% increase" is a genetic-instrument scaling, not a dietary recommendation.

**Chronic kidney disease (CKD).** Meta-analyses of observational cohorts report roughly a 13% lower incidence of CKD associated with coffee drinking, with dose-dependent benefit at ≥2 cups/day. Associations appear stronger in women in some analyses, and results for eGFR decline are inconsistent. This evidence is observational and cannot establish causation.

**Gallstones.** In the Health Professionals Follow-up Study (46,008 men), the primary baseline-intake analysis linked 2–3 cups/day to about 21% lower symptomatic gallstone risk (RR 0.79) and ≥4 cups to about 33% lower (RR 0.67); a secondary "consistent consumption" subgroup showed up to ~40% lower (RR 0.60), so the ~40% figure is the optimistic end. In women (Nurses' Health Study, 80,898), ≥4 cups/day was linked to ~28% lower risk. Only caffeinated coffee showed benefit, and HPFS is men-only. A 2020 MR study (Journal of Internal Medicine) supported a causal, caffeine-driven protective association (genetic OR ~0.89 per cup/day), but later MR evidence is mixed: a 2022 MR found no significant causal effect of coffee consumption itself (only of caffeine metabolism) and even linked genetically predicted decaffeinated coffee to higher risk, and a 2021 MR found protection only after adjusting for BMI and smoking. So the association is robust and points toward caffeine-driven protection, but the causal claim is not settled.

**Multiple sclerosis.** Two matched case-control studies (Swedish: 1,620 cases; US: 1,159 cases) found high intake (>900 mL/day) associated with ~30% lower MS risk (OR ~0.69–0.70). A 2025 meta-analysis pooled OR ~0.78. These are observational designs subject to recall bias and reverse causation.

**DNA damage.** A 4-week single-blind RCT (100 healthy adults) found that 500 mL/day of dark-roast coffee was associated with about a 23% reduction in spontaneous DNA strand breaks (comet assay). Importantly, that ~23% is the within-coffee-group change from baseline, not a 23% reduction versus the water control; the between-group (coffee vs water) comparison was far weaker. In 2020 the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) reviewed this and related trials and concluded that a cause-and-effect relationship between the coffee blend and protection of DNA from strand breaks "has not been established," citing failure to replicate (a near-identical replication from the same lab found no significant between-group difference) and no plausible mechanism. The work came from a single research group and underpinned a commercial health-claim application, so it should be read as a preliminary, contested, regulator-rejected result rather than an established interventional benefit.

**Melanoma/skin.** In the NIH-AARP cohort, ≥4 cups/day of caffeinated coffee was associated with HR 0.75 (0.64–0.89) for invasive malignant melanoma; decaffeinated showed no significant association. A meta-analysis pooled RR 0.81 for caffeinated coffee, with high between-study heterogeneity (I²~64%). Caveats: the inverse association applied only to invasive melanoma (for melanoma in situ the HR was non-protective, 1.11), the single cohort lacked individual-level sun/UV exposure and phenotype data (fair skin, freckling, family history — the strongest melanoma confounders), and replication is mixed (a Norwegian cohort found no significant association for total coffee).

**Hearing/tinnitus.** In the Nurses' Health Study II (65,085 women, 18-year follow-up), higher caffeine intake was associated with lower incident tinnitus — about 15% lower at 450–599 mg/day and 21% lower at >600 mg/day. This is an observational association.

**Bowel recovery after surgery.** A 2022 meta-analysis of 6 small, mostly unblinded RCTs (416 patients) reported that coffee shortened time to first defecation after colorectal surgery by about 15 hours (mean difference −15.0 h, 95% CI −17.8 to −12.3; I²=0%), with no significant effect on flatus or length of stay. The effect size is inconsistent across analyses, however: a larger 2024 meta-analysis (8 studies, 610 patients) found only a modest effect (SMD −0.52, 95% CI −1.00 to −0.03) with high heterogeneity (I²=88%) and rated the evidence LOW certainty by GRADE, while another estimated closer to ~10 hours. More recent blinded, placebo-controlled trials are largely null — the double-blind CaCo trial (2022) found only a 0.8 h difference versus placebo (P=0.774, stopped for futility) and a 2023 three-arm RCT (water vs decaf vs caffeinated coffee) found no difference (p=0.53). This is randomized evidence, but weaker and more uncertain than the single 15-hour figure suggests.

**Alertness/productivity.** RCTs consistently show caffeine improves reaction time, vigilance, and subjective alertness, especially when baseline alertness is low (e.g., sleep deprivation, morning grogginess) — a well-established acute pharmacological effect.

**Oral/dental and eye health (mixed/cautionary).** Evidence on periodontitis is contradictory (pooled ORs 1.03–1.14, essentially null); sugar-sweetened instant coffee is linked to tooth loss, implicating additives rather than coffee itself. For glaucoma, heavy intake (≥3 cups/day) has been associated with *increased* risk of exfoliation glaucoma and high-tension primary open-angle glaucoma among people with a family history; caffeine transiently raises intraocular pressure (~1 mmHg).

## Strength of Evidence

The designs that best limit confounding here are still individually fragile. The kidney-stone and gallstone MR studies hint at causality but are single or contested and lean on pleiotropic caffeine-metabolism instruments, so they suggest rather than prove a causal protective effect. The post-operative ileus and DNA-damage findings are genuinely randomized, but the trials are small, mostly unblinded, inconsistent across meta-analyses, and — for DNA — judged by EFSA not to establish cause and effect; more recent blinded surgical trials are largely null. Gout, CKD, melanoma, hearing, and MS evidence is observational — sometimes large and consistent, but unable to rule out reverse causation, residual confounding, or healthy-user bias. Oral-health and glaucoma findings are weak, inconsistent, or point the "wrong" way, and should be presented as cautions rather than benefits. Social/productivity claims rest on acute caffeine pharmacology, not long-term outcome trials.

## Mechanisms

Plausible mechanisms include: caffeine's xanthine-oxidase inhibition and uricosuric (urine-increasing) effects (gout, stones); chlorogenic acids and other polyphenols providing antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity (CKD, melanoma, DNA protection); caffeine-stimulated gallbladder contraction and reduced biliary cholesterol (gallstones); adenosine-receptor antagonism with neuroprotective, anti-inflammatory cytokine effects (MS, alertness); and coffee's pro-motility/colonic stimulation (bowel recovery). Most mechanisms are demonstrated in vitro or in animals and remain hypotheses in humans.

## Practical Notes

Moderate consumption (about 3–5 cups/day, ≤400 mg caffeine) is considered safe for most healthy adults by authorities such as EFSA and the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. Several of the associations above (gout, melanoma, gallstones) track with *caffeinated* coffee, while sugar and creamer additions may negate dental or metabolic benefits. People with a family history of glaucoma, those who are pregnant, or those sensitive to caffeine should be cautious. None of these findings justify starting coffee for medical reasons; they are reassuring context for existing drinkers, not established treatments.
