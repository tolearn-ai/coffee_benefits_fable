# Nutritional Composition & Antioxidants

## Overview

Coffee is far more than a caffeine delivery system. A brewed cup is a dilute but chemically rich infusion of plant phenolics, roast-generated polymers, an alkaloid called trigonelline, and trace micronutrients. Because most adults drink it daily and in volume, coffee is consistently identified in dietary surveys as the single largest contributor of antioxidants to the diets of many Western and East Asian populations—not because it is the most antioxidant-dense food per gram, but because of how much of it people drink. This report summarizes what is known about coffee's nutritional and antioxidant composition, separating in-vitro/chemistry findings from human evidence.

## Key Findings

**Chlorogenic acids (CGAs).** CGAs are coffee's dominant polyphenols, comprising roughly 4–8% of green Arabica and 7–14% of green Robusta dry matter (species-dependent; broadly about 4–14% by weight). A typical cup delivers on the order of 70–350 mg of chlorogenic acids, with wide variation by roast, bean species, and brew method; both caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee contain them. Roasting progressively degrades CGAs, so lighter roasts retain more, while espresso's high coffee-to-water ratio concentrates them per milliliter. (Chemistry/analytical reviews; Phenol-Explorer database.)

**Coffee as a leading dietary antioxidant source.** A widely cited 2005 University of Scranton analysis (Joe Vinson), presented at an American Chemical Society national meeting, ranked coffee as the No. 1 source of antioxidants in the U.S. diet on the combined basis of antioxidant content per serving and consumption frequency, ahead of black tea and bananas. The ranking is driven by how much coffee Americans drink rather than by coffee being the most antioxidant-rich food per serving (Vinson noted dates rank highest per serving alone), and the measurement was in-vitro antioxidant content. A cross-sectional study of Norwegian women reported that coffee explained roughly 54% of the variation in total dietary antioxidant intake, versus about 22% for fruits and vegetables. These are intake/exposure rankings, not proof of health outcomes—and as Vinson himself cautioned, high antioxidant levels in a beverage do not necessarily translate into levels measured in the body.

**Melanoidins.** During roasting, Maillard and caramelization reactions generate brown, high-molecular-weight polymers called melanoidins, which can account for up to about 25% of a coffee brew's dry matter (typical range ~10–25%). They form predominantly during roasting and increase from light to dark roast, partially offsetting the CGA losses caused by roasting; small amounts of melanoidin-like precursor structures have, however, been described in green beans (attributed to Maillard products generated during postharvest drying), so they are not strictly absent before roasting. In vitro they show antioxidant, metal-chelating, and prebiotic/dietary-fiber-like activity—but this is chemical activity measured in the lab rather than a demonstrated physiological effect, and antioxidant capacity is not uniform across roast levels.

**Trigonelline and micronutrients.** Trigonelline makes up roughly 1–3% of green-coffee dry weight (real-world measurements usually fall near the lower end, around 1%) and is partially converted to niacin (nicotinic acid, vitamin B3) during roasting, making coffee a modest B3 source. Per 8-oz (240 mL) cup, coffee supplies small amounts of micronutrients—approximately 11% of the Daily Value for riboflavin (B2), ~3% DV niacin, ~7 mg magnesium (~2% RDA), and ~116 mg potassium (~2–3%). These are minor contributions individually but add up for heavy drinkers.

**Anti-inflammatory effects.** Evidence is genuinely mixed. A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Nutrients* (10 articles reporting 11 study populations, all cross-sectional, 61,047 participants) found **no statistically significant overall association** between coffee intake and C-reactive protein (CRP): a dose-response mean change of −2.4% per 100 mL/day (95% CI −8.7% to +4.4%, p = 0.49). The pooled subgroups (by sex and geography) were all non-significant; the divergence appeared among the largest individual studies—European and U.S. women and Japanese men trended toward lower CRP (~1.3–5.5% per 100 mL/day), whereas European men trended toward higher CRP. Importantly, this null result is one finding within a mixed literature: an earlier 2018 meta-analysis found coffee significantly associated with *lower* CRP, and a 2024 meta-analysis (11 cross-sectional studies, 66,691 subjects) reported a significant inverse linear association. Claims that coffee broadly "lowers inflammation" still overstate the evidence, which remains inconsistent and entirely observational.

**Milk + coffee polyphenol interaction.** A small human crossover study (Duarte & Farah, 2011; ~9 subjects) found 24-hour urinary chlorogenic-acid recovery fell from 68% ± 20% (coffee alone) to 40% ± 27% (coffee dissolved in whole milk), and the authors hedged that milk *may impair* bioavailability. This rests on a single, small trial that used an extreme milk load (coffee dissolved in whole milk, not a normal splash) and measured urinary recovery—a proxy that can reflect altered metabolism or excretion rather than true absorption (a published correction exists). The human evidence is itself split: a comparable crossover study (Renouf et al., 2010, Nestlé) found adding whole milk did *not* change the plasma appearance of coffee phenolic acids. In-vitro findings are likewise nuanced rather than simply favorable: adding milk first causes an immediate decrease via protein binding, and any net *increase* in bioaccessibility is fat-dependent, emerging only after simulated digestion with whole or semi-skimmed milk (skimmed milk gave no increase). The net physiological effect of adding milk remains genuinely unresolved.

## Strength of Evidence

The *compositional* claims (CGA content, melanoidin formation, trigonelline-to-niacin conversion, micronutrient amounts) rest on robust analytical chemistry and are well established, though specific magnitudes vary by species, roast, and brew. The claim that coffee is a top dietary antioxidant source is well supported as a statement about *intake*, but high antioxidant content in a beverage does not reliably translate into elevated antioxidant activity in the body. The anti-inflammatory and bioavailability findings are observational or small-mechanistic; they show inconsistent associations and plausible mechanisms, **not demonstrated causation**. The milk-interaction evidence is conflicting across in-vitro and human studies and should be treated as unsettled.

## Mechanisms

Coffee's phenolics (chlorogenic, caffeic, and ferulic acids) act as direct radical scavengers and metal chelators, and may modulate inflammatory signaling (e.g., NF-κB) and oxidative-stress pathways. Melanoidins contribute antioxidant and chelating capacity and may exert prebiotic effects in the colon. CGAs are extensively metabolized by gut microbiota into smaller phenolic acids that are absorbed and may mediate downstream effects. Milk proteins can form hydrogen-bonded and hydrophobic complexes with CGAs, plausibly altering their absorption kinetics—the proposed basis for the reduced-recovery finding, though the magnitude and direction of the net effect are disputed.

## Practical Notes

- Lighter roasts and larger brewed volumes deliver more chlorogenic acid; darker roasts trade some CGA for melanoidins, so "more antioxidants" depends on which compound you mean.
- Both caffeinated and decaf coffee provide CGAs and melanoidins, useful for those limiting caffeine.
- Adding milk *may* blunt chlorogenic-acid bioavailability, but the evidence is mixed (human and in-vitro studies disagree) and any effect is unlikely to negate coffee's contribution; this is not a strong reason to drink it black.
- Coffee's micronutrients are a bonus, not a primary source—it should not replace nutrient-dense foods.
- These are nutritional-composition findings; they do not by themselves establish disease-prevention benefits.
