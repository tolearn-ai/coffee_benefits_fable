# Physical & Athletic Performance

## Overview

Among all the purported benefits of coffee, its effect on physical and athletic performance is unusual: it rests not on observational epidemiology but on a large body of randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials. The active ergogenic compound is caffeine, which coffee delivers in substantial doses (roughly 80–100 mg per 8 oz cup, though content varies widely). Caffeine is one of the most studied and well-supported legal performance aids. The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) reaffirmed in its 2021 position stand that caffeine acutely enhances many forms of exercise performance, while emphasizing that responses vary between individuals. This report focuses on caffeine as the active agent, noting where coffee specifically has been tested.

## Key Findings

**Endurance.** The largest and most consistent benefits appear in aerobic endurance and time-to-exhaustion tasks. A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis (Southward et al., *Sports Medicine*) reported that caffeine improved time-trial mean power output by about 3.0% (effect size ≈ 0.23) and reduced time-trial completion time by about 2.2% (effect size ≈ 0.41). These are small average effects with wide individual variability — the standard deviation on the power-output gain actually exceeds its mean, a handful of participants got slower or weaker after caffeine, and the pooled data are dominated by trained, mostly male cyclists — so they are best read as a small average benefit rather than a reliable ~3%/~2% gain for any given athlete. Meta-analyses of running time-to-exhaustion show a medium effect (Hedges' g ≈ 0.39). An umbrella review by Grgic et al. (2019) found moderate effect sizes for aerobic tasks and muscular endurance across published meta-analyses.

**Muscular endurance, strength, and power.** Effects on resistance-type outcomes are smaller but still positive. Grgic et al. (2018, *JISSN*) found caffeine improved maximal muscle strength (SMD ≈ 0.20) and muscle power (SMD ≈ 0.17) — small effects by conventional standards. The strength benefit was statistically significant for upper-body strength (SMD ≈ 0.21) but not for lower-body strength (SMD ≈ 0.15). Two of these results are statistically fragile: the power effect was borderline (p ≈ 0.047) and lost significance after correction for publication bias, and the lower-body null reflects "no detectable effect in this dataset" rather than established absence — free-weight squat studies and some later analyses do show small lower-body gains. Muscular endurance shows a reliable small-to-moderate effect (SMD ≈ 0.30; 95% CI 0.21–0.38).

**Perceived exertion.** Caffeine consistently lowers the rating of perceived exertion (RPE). A classic meta-analysis (Doherty & Smith, 2005) found RPE reduced by roughly 5.6% during constant-load exercise (effect size ≈ −0.47). The same analysis reported that RPE during exercise statistically accounted for about 29% of the *variance* in caffeine's performance improvement across studies — a cross-study correlation (an R²) that the authors said "may partly explain" the ergogenic effect, not a causal partition or a fixed share of the benefit. Later evidence complicates even this association: caffeine sometimes improves performance without lowering RPE, and expectancy/placebo effects can move RPE on their own.

**Dosing.** The ISSN concludes caffeine reliably improves performance at **3–6 mg per kg of body mass**; the minimal effective dose is uncertain but may be as low as **2 mg/kg**. The dose-response is not perfectly flat across this range or across sports — some endurance work finds low doses (≤3 mg/kg) among the most effective, while 3 mg/kg underperforms 6 mg/kg on some measures. Very high doses (e.g., **9 mg/kg**) do not add benefit over the moderate range and markedly increase side effects (jitteriness, GI distress, elevated heart rate, impaired sleep), though they remain ergogenic versus placebo and the plateau shifts upward in heavily caffeine-habituated athletes.

**Timing and form.** Caffeine is typically ingested about **60 minutes before exercise** (anhydrous capsules peak in plasma around 45–60 min). Alternative delivery forms — caffeinated gum, gels, and mouth rinses — can act faster. Coffee itself has also been shown to be ergogenic in trials comparing caffeinated coffee to decaf/placebo.

## Strength of Evidence

This is one of the few coffee-related domains where **causation, not just association, is supported**. The findings above derive overwhelmingly from randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trials and meta-analyses of such trials, not from observational cohorts. That design controls for the confounding and reverse-causation problems that plague observational nutrition research, so it is fair to say caffeine *causes* acute performance improvements in controlled settings.

Important caveats remain. Effect sizes are generally small to moderate and vary by task — larger for endurance, smaller for maximal strength/power. Blinding integrity is often unverified and is frequently broken: when tested, participants can correctly guess whether they received caffeine, correct identification is linked to larger gains, and placebo/expectancy alone improves performance by roughly 1–3%, which somewhat softens clean attribution to caffeine's pharmacology in trials that do not verify blinding. Responses also differ between individuals, partly due to genetic variation in caffeine metabolism (e.g., the *CYP1A2* gene) and habitual intake, though the practical predictive value of genotype testing is still debated. Most evidence comes from trained young men; data in women, older adults, and recreational exercisers are thinner. Finally, much research uses pure caffeine rather than coffee, and coffee's other compounds and variable caffeine content add noise — so extrapolation from "caffeine" to "a cup of coffee" is reasonable but not exact.

## Mechanisms

The dominant mechanism at physiological doses is **central**: caffeine antagonizes adenosine A1 and A2A receptors in the brain. By blocking adenosine (which normally promotes drowsiness and dampens neural activity), caffeine reduces central fatigue, increases arousal, motivation, and dopaminergic/noradrenergic activity, and lowers perceived exertion and pain. Secondary, mostly higher-dose or peripheral mechanisms include modulation of ryanodine receptors to enhance calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (improving muscle contraction), phosphodiesterase inhibition, improved neuromuscular transmission and motor-unit recruitment, and regulation of extracellular potassium. An older "glycogen-sparing/fat-oxidation" hypothesis is now considered a minor contributor rather than the main driver.

## Practical Notes

- A practical dose is roughly 3 mg/kg (about 200 mg for a 70 kg person, ≈2 cups of brewed coffee), taken ~45–60 minutes before activity.
- More is not better: doses near 6 mg/kg are the upper end of the reliably ergogenic range; 9 mg/kg adds side effects without added benefit.
- Coffee is a legitimate and convenient caffeine source, but caffeine content per cup varies substantially, making precise dosing harder than with capsules.
- Caffeine's mild diuretic effect does not cause meaningful dehydration during exercise at typical doses.
- Caffeine can disrupt sleep for hours; afternoon/evening use before training should be weighed against next-day recovery.
- Caffeine is permitted by WADA but is on its monitoring program; very high intakes may draw scrutiny in elite sport.
- Individuals should test their personal tolerance, as anxiety, GI upset, and sleep disruption vary widely.
